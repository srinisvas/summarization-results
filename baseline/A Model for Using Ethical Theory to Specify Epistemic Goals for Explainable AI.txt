The paper discusses the normative requirements for "explainable AI" (XAI) from a moral accountability perspective. It argues that current XAI approaches, which focus on epistemic accessibility of AI inner workings, are insufficient to address the challenge of holding AI accountable for their morally problematic actions. Instead, it proposes using Nomy Arpaly's account of moral-worthiness, which involves assessing whether an entity can respond to morally relevant reasons. This account provides three key epistemic goals for XAI tools: (1) the ability to perceive morally relevant information, (2) the ability to distinguish between morally relevant and irrelevant features of behavior, and (3) the ability to use those morally relevant features to motivate actions. These goals are also practical constraints on the development of XAI tools, ruling out certain approaches that do not adequately address the need for fine-grained, context-dependent interpretations. The paper concludes that focusing on these specific epistemic and practical requirements is crucial for bridging the gap between AI and human moral accountability, ensuring that AI can be held responsible for their actions. The paper emphasizes the importance of AI XAI tools that provide detailed, human-interpretable insights into AI decision-making processes to facilitate accountability in AI systems}
