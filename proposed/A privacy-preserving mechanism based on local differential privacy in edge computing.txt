**Executive Summary**

This paper addresses the challenge of privacy-preserving location data collection in edge computing environments, where traditional privacy mechanisms are insufficient due to the inherent limitations of lightweight devices. The authors propose a novel LDP (Local Differential Privacy) mechanism based on Voronoi diagrams, which effectively balances user privacy with data utility. The proposed method divides the road network into Voronoi grids, assigns users to these grids, and introduces random noise to their locations within each grid. This approach ensures that users' real locations remain private, while still allowing for the collection of useful data for service providers.

Through comparison experiments, the paper demonstrates that the proposed LDP mechanism outperforms classical privacy-preserving methods in terms of both privacy protection and data availability. The use of Voronoi diagrams allows for efficient and scalable location data collection, making it suitable for the decentralized nature of edge computing. The authors also highlight the importance of integrating privacy-preserving mechanisms with edge computing to meet the growing demand for lightweight and efficient data processing.

The work concludes by emphasizing the need for further research into enhancing the privacy-preserving model to better align with the unique characteristics of edge computing. Additionally, the authors suggest exploring the intersection of edge computing and blockchain to develop more effective privacy mechanisms. The proposed framework not only addresses the privacy concerns of location data but also contributes to the broader goal of ensuring data utility and regulatory compliance in decentralized systems.
